CORNELL UNIVERSITY was founded by Ezra Cornell, of Ithaca, and Andrew Dickson White, of Syracuse. The pair met in the New York State Senate and combined their education ideals. Put briefly, White believed in the need for a university in the U.S. that would take a new approach to education, and Cornell wanted to use his money for the greatest good.

When Cornell's charter was signed on April 27, 1865, it was a tumultuous time in history. Just the day before Gov. Reuben Fenton signed the charter in his gas-lit chambers in Albany, Abraham Lincoln's funeral procession traveled through the streets of Albany.

While Cornell was founded in 1865, its Inauguration Day took place Oct. 7, 1868. After passing an entrance examination, 412 applicants were accepted, which was the largest entering class in American history at that point.

Cornell's charter explicitly established the university as non-sectarian and co-educational, welcoming anyone who was "academically qualified regardless of sex, color, creed or national origins."

The colors on Campbell's soups were inspired by the university after a company executive, Herb Williams, attended the annual Cornell-Penn football game in 1898. Williams was "so taken with Cornell's brilliant new red-and-white uniforms" that he convinced Campbell to use the colors.

The chicken nugget has roots at Cornell. Robert C. Baker (1921-2006), a Lansing native and Cornell professor, invented the chicken nugget and more than 40 other poultry-related items. Baker founded the university's Institute of Food Science and Marketing. In addition to the chicken nugget, Baker revolutionized the way to bind breading to chicken, co-invented a machine that de-bones chicken and created the chicken and turkey hot dogs and turkey ham. Naturally, Baker is a member of the American Poultry Hall of Fame.
